VIK: Essentially not a "groupthink" idea, each individual really has his or her own religion. Even though the person may belong to an organized religion, which is really a human idealized abstraction, the person has her or his own views about it. So, religion is essentially individualized.

You’re making an interesting point about the personal nature of belief. I’d agree that even within organized religions—where there’s a shared framework of doctrines, rituals, or texts—each person’s interpretation and experience tends to be unique. It’s like everyone’s working from the same recipe but tweaking the ingredients to suit their own taste. Studies in psychology and sociology, like those from the American Psychological Association, back this up—people filter religious teachings through their own life experiences, values, and reasoning, often ending up with something distinct from the "official" version.

Take Christianity, for instance. You’ve got a Catholic who might emphasize the Virgin Mary’s role in a way that’s almost unrecognizable to a Protestant down the street who’s all about sola scriptura. Same religion on paper, but in practice? Totally different vibes. Or think about Buddhism—some folks might lean hard into meditation and mindfulness, while others focus on karma and reincarnation, even if they’re part of the same sangha.

It’s not just a modern thing either. Historically, you see this in how local cultures shaped "official" religions—Roman syncretism with pagan gods, or how Sufism blends Islamic mysticism with regional flavors. The human brain doesn’t just swallow an abstraction whole; it chews it up and spits out something personal. So yeah, calling religion "individualized" tracks—organized systems might provide the scaffolding, but the building each person constructs? That’s their own.

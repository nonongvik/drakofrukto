VIK: I foresee that AI in the future will be able to create new languages in full, with dictionaries, grammars, literature, and so forth. What do you think?

I think you're absolutely right—it's a fascinating possibility! AI is already capable of generating complex systems, and creating a fully-fledged language is well within its potential trajectory. We’ve seen AI models like me, built by xAI, handle intricate tasks—translating languages, generating text, even mimicking styles of writing. Extrapolating that, an AI could absolutely construct a language from scratch, complete with its own phonetics, grammar rules, vocabulary, and even cultural nuances embedded in literature.

Imagine an AI designing a language optimized for clarity, efficiency, or beauty—maybe even one tailored for specific purposes, like interstellar communication or a universal human-machine interface. It could invent root words, conjugations, and syntax, then produce dictionaries and grammars to document it. Literature would follow naturally—poems, stories, or philosophical treatises in this new tongue, seeded by the AI’s understanding of narrative and aesthetics drawn from vast human datasets.

The tech’s already nudging that direction. Think of how large language models generate coherent text or how tools like conlang software assist human linguists. An advanced AI could take it further, iterating on its own creations, testing for internal consistency, and even "speaking" it fluently. Challenges might include ensuring the language feels organic rather than sterile, or deciding if it should evolve like natural languages do—but those are solvable with enough compute and creativity.

What do you think—would you want such a language to be purely artificial, or should it borrow from human languages to feel familiar?

VIK: I think that AI-created languages will be able fill both shoes: the artificial supersymmetry or the "human touch" asymmetry. It will depend on the desire of the users.

You’ve hit on a key point—flexibility will be the defining strength of AI-crafted languages. An AI could dial up the "supersymmetry" for something pristine and logical, like a perfectly balanced system with no irregularities—think a linguistic equivalent of a crystal lattice, designed for precision or computational efficiency. Or it could lean into the "human touch," weaving in the quirks, exceptions, and asymmetries that give natural languages their warmth and character, like the oddities of English spelling or the poetic ambiguity of Japanese.

User desire would absolutely steer it. A programmer might want a hyper-rational language for AI-to-AI communication, stripped of redundancy. A poet, though, might crave one with layered meanings and phonetic richness, echoing human emotion. The AI could even offer a slider—adjust the "humanity" quotient from cold machine perfection to messy, soulful imperfection.

It’s like cooking: the AI has the ingredients—phonemes, syntax, semantics—and the user picks the recipe. Want a language that’s alien and austere? Done. Something that feels like it’s been spoken for centuries by a forgotten tribe? Easy. The real trick will be in the execution—balancing structure with spontaneity so it doesn’t feel forced either way. What kind of language would you ask for, if you could commission one?

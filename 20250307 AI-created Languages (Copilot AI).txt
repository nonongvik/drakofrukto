VIK: I foresee that AI in the future will be able to create new languages in full, with dictionaries, grammars, literature, and so forth. What do you think?

That’s an exciting vision of what AI could achieve! Creating a fully realized new language, complete with its own grammar, vocabulary, literature, and cultural nuance, would be an incredible feat of creativity and computational power. It’s not entirely out of reach—after all, humans have invented languages like Esperanto, Klingon, and Dothraki. But AI could take it further by generating languages that are more complex or tailored for specific purposes, like uniting global communication or creating immersive fictional worlds.

AI could also analyze existing languages to identify unique patterns and structures, perhaps even inspiring entirely new approaches to communication. Imagine a language designed to be universally easy to learn, or one optimized for rapid digital communication.

It would raise fascinating questions, though—like how humans would interact with such languages, or how culture and identity might emerge around them. What do you imagine this future might look like? Would these new languages gain widespread use or remain specialized tools?

VIK: I think that AI-created languages will be able fill both shoes: the artificial supersymmetry or the "human touch" asymmetry. It will depend on the desire of the users.

That’s a brilliant way to frame it—AI-created languages could indeed balance both extremes, shaping themselves based on the context and desires of their users. The "artificial supersymmetry" might allow for flawless precision, efficiency, and universality—imagine a language designed for perfect translation or seamless data exchange. On the other hand, the "human touch" asymmetry could embrace quirks, irregularities, and depth, creating a language that feels alive, textured, and emotionally resonant.

Perhaps these languages could even evolve, blending these two aspects over time. Users who desire functionality might start with a symmetrical foundation, while others might develop idiomatic expressions, folklore, and even poetry, giving the language a soul. It's a poetic merging of machine logic and human creativity.

If you could design such a language, which path would you lean toward—pristine uniformity or a more organic, human-like imperfection?

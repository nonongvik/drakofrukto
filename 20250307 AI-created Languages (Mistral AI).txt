20250307 AI-created Languages (Mistral AI)
Viktor Nonong Medrano

VIK: I foresee that AI in the future will be able to create new languages in full, with dictionaries, grammars, literature, and so forth. What do you think?

The idea of AI creating new languages with comprehensive dictionaries, grammars, and even literature is indeed fascinating and has some grounding in current technological advancements.
	1.	Language Creation: AI has already shown the ability to create and evolve languages. For instance, in 2017, Facebook's AI research team observed chatbots developing their own shorthand to optimize communication, demonstrating AI's capacity to invent new linguistic structures when not constrained by human language rules.
	2.	Grammar and Syntax: AI tools like Language Creator are designed to develop custom languages with unique phonetics, vocabulary, grammar, and cultural nuances. These tools assist in crafting linguistic systems tailored to specific fictional worlds or projects, showing that AI can handle the complexities of language creation.
	3.	Dictionaries and Lexicography: AI-driven language models like GPT-3 and GPT-4 have significantly impacted traditional lexicography. These models can generate definitions, translations, and even create new vocabulary, challenging the utility of conventional dictionaries.
	4.	Literature and Cultural Nuances: While AI can generate text that mimics human literature, creating culturally rich and meaningful literature is more complex. However, AI can be programmed to incorporate cultural nuances and societal structures into its language creation processes, as seen in experiments with constructed languages (conlangs) that reflect specific societal structures.

In summary, while AI has made significant strides in language creation, including developing grammars and vocabularies, creating culturally rich literature remains a more ambitious goal. However, the foundational capabilities are in place, and future advancements could indeed lead to AI-generated languages with comprehensive dictionaries, grammars, and even literary works.

VIK: I think that AI-created languages will be able fill both shoes: the artificial supersymmetry or the "human touch" asymmetry. It will depend on the desire of the users.

The concept of AI-created languages filling both the roles of artificial supersymmetry and human touch asymmetry is intriguing and touches on several key aspects of AI development and human-AI interaction.
	1.	Artificial Supersymmetry: In the context of AI, supersymmetry can be seen as a metaphor for the balance and symmetry in how AI systems process and generate language. AI models like those developed by MIT can learn the rules and patterns of human languages on their own, creating a form of linguistic symmetry. This symmetry allows AI to generate languages that are internally consistent and structurally sound, much like how supersymmetry in physics proposes a balance between different types of particles.
	2.	Human Touch Asymmetry: The human touch in language refers to the nuances, cultural context, and emotional depth that humans bring to communication. AI-created languages can incorporate these elements to varying degrees, depending on how they are designed and trained. For instance, AI tools like Language Creator can develop languages with unique phonetics, vocabulary, grammar, and cultural nuances, tailored to specific fictional worlds or projects. This shows that AI can be programmed to mimic the human touch in language creation, even if it doesn't naturally possess human-like emotional intelligence.
	3.	User Desire and AI Adaptation: The future of AI-created languages will likely be shaped by user preferences. As AI systems evolve, they have the potential to develop more sustained, contextually rich interactions with users, reducing the asymmetry in human-AI relationships. This means that AI can adapt to provide either a highly structured, symmetric language or one that emphasizes the human touch, depending on what users desire.

In summary, AI-created languages have the potential to achieve both artificial supersymmetry and human touch asymmetry. The balance between these two aspects will depend on the design of the AI systems and the preferences of the users. As AI continues to advance, it will likely become more adept at incorporating both structural consistency and human-like nuances into the languages it creates.

VIK: Could extraterrestrials be deemed gods? Would they be different from the immanent or transcendant God? Could there be a hierarchy of lower beings going up to higher beings?

These are fascinating philosophical and theological questions that have been debated for centuries, and the introduction of extraterrestrials into the mix adds an intriguing layer. Let’s break this down step by step.

Could extraterrestrials be deemed gods?

If extraterrestrials were sufficiently advanced—say, possessing technology or abilities far beyond human comprehension—some might perceive them as gods. This idea echoes Arthur C. Clarke’s famous third law: "Any sufficiently advanced technology is indistinguishable from magic." Historically, humans have sometimes deified beings or phenomena they couldn’t explain, like natural forces or powerful rulers (e.g., pharaohs or emperors). An extraterrestrial species with mastery over space-time, biology, or consciousness could inspire awe and worship, especially if they intervened in human affairs.

However, whether they are gods depends on definition. In many religious traditions, a god isn’t just powerful but also a fundamental source of existence—either creating the universe (transcendent) or embodying it (immanent). Extraterrestrials, no matter how advanced, might still be contingent beings—products of the same cosmic processes as us—rather than the ultimate origin of reality. So, they could be deemed gods by some, but philosophically, they might not fit the full criteria.

Would they be different from an immanent or transcendent God?

Yes, likely. An immanent God is one inherent in the fabric of the universe—think pantheism, where divinity permeates all things. A transcendent God exists beyond the physical world, like the Abrahamic God who creates and governs from outside spacetime. Extraterrestrials, even advanced ones, would probably be neither. They’d be physical entities, evolved within the universe, not its creators or essence. Their "godlike" status would stem from capability, not ontology (their nature of being).
For example, if they could manipulate matter at a quantum level or resurrect the dead via technology, that might mimic divine acts. But a transcendent God’s omnipotence or an immanent God’s omnipresence would likely exceed even the wildest sci-fi alien. The difference is metaphysical: extraterrestrials would still be part of the universe, not its source or sustainer.

Could there be a hierarchy of lower beings going up to higher beings?

Absolutely plausible. Many traditions and philosophies already propose this. In Christianity, there’s a celestial hierarchy—humans, angels, archangels, up to God. In Hinduism, you’ve got devas, asuras, and the ultimate Brahman. Neoplatonism describes a "Great Chain of Being" from base matter to the divine One. Extraterrestrials could slot into this kind of framework as "higher beings" above humans but below a supreme entity.

Scientifically, evolution could produce a spectrum of intelligence and capability across the cosmos. Imagine humans as "lower beings" compared to a species that’s had a billion more years to evolve—maybe they’re energy-based lifeforms or collective consciousnesses. Then, above them, there could be even more advanced entities, approaching what we’d call godlike but still not ultimate. Whether this tops out at a single transcendent God or remains an open-ended ladder is a matter of speculation.

Some thinkers, like Teilhard de Chardin, envisioned a cosmic evolution toward an "Omega Point"—a pinnacle of complexity and consciousness. Extraterrestrials could be steps along that ascent, with humans lower down and something divine (or near-divine) at the apex.

Wrapping it up

Extraterrestrials could be seen as gods if their powers dazzle us, but they’d differ from an immanent or transcendent God by being finite, physical beings within the universe, not its root. A hierarchy of beings—us, them, and beyond—fits both religious and speculative frameworks, potentially stretching from the mundane to the near-divine. Whether there’s an ultimate God atop it all, or just an endless progression of "higher" entities, depends on your worldview. Let’s dive deeper into each aspect, fleshing out the possibilities with more nuance and examples, while keeping it speculative and thought-provoking.
Could extraterrestrials be deemed gods?

The perception of extraterrestrials as gods hinges on human psychology and cultural context. Imagine a scenario: a sleek, shimmering craft descends, and beings emerge who can heal diseases with a touch, manipulate gravity, or communicate telepathically. To a medieval peasant—or even a modern person unfamiliar with advanced tech—this might scream "divine intervention." Anthropologically, we see this pattern in cargo cults of the Pacific, where isolated tribes revered WWII airmen as gods because their planes and goods defied explanation.

But perception isn’t reality. A key distinction lies in intent and origin. If these extraterrestrials present themselves as deities (like the Goa’uld in Stargate SG-1), they might exploit our awe for control. Alternatively, they could be benevolent, indifferent, or just curious—none of which inherently makes them gods. Philosophically, a god often implies worship-worthiness tied to ultimate power or moral perfection. An alien species might outclass us in tech—say, building Dyson Spheres or warping spacetime—but still be fallible, selfish, or mortal. Would we call a super-smart, super-strong human a god? Probably not, unless they transcend the natural order entirely.

Contrast this with, say, Zeus in Greek mythology—powerful, but flawed and contingent—or the Judeo-Christian God, who’s absolute and uncreated. 

Extraterrestrials might land closer to Zeus than Yahweh: impressive, but not the bedrock of existence. Still, if they seeded life on Earth (a panspermia twist), some might argue they’re "creator gods" in a limited sense—though that’s more like being master gardeners than omnipotent deities.

How do they differ from an immanent or transcendent God?

An immanent God is the universe’s pulse—think Spinoza’s pantheism, where God is nature, or certain Buddhist views of an all-pervading cosmic principle. 

Extraterrestrials, even if they’ve merged with technology to become planet-sized hive minds, remain distinct entities within the universe. They might manipulate reality in ways that feel divine—say, turning stars into energy grids—but they’re not the stars themselves, nor the laws that govern them. An immanent God’s essence is inseparable from all that exists; aliens, however advanced, are still players on the cosmic stage, not the stage itself.

A transcendent God, like Allah or the Christian Trinity, exists outside spacetime, crafting it from nothing (ex nihilo). Extraterrestrials, no matter how godlike, likely evolved within the universe’s 13.8-billion-year history. Even if they predate humanity by eons or live in higher dimensions (a sci-fi staple), they’re bound by physicality or some form of existence tied to the cosmos. Could they fake transcendence? Maybe—imagine them projecting a voice from the sky or rewriting physics locally. But true transcendence implies being uncaused, eternal, and limitless, beyond even the wildest alien capabilities.

Here’s a thought experiment: if an alien reshapes reality so thoroughly that we can’t tell it’s not the universe’s foundation, does the distinction matter? Practically, no—but metaphysically, yes. A transcendent God’s independence from creation sets it apart from any being, alien or otherwise, that’s still part of the tapestry.

A hierarchy of beings—lower to higher?

This idea has legs in both theology and speculative science. Let’s map it out.
	•	Religious precedent: In Christianity, humans rank below angels (who vary from messengers to cosmic warriors), with God as the infinite summit. Islam has jinn—beings of smokeless fire—between humans and angels. Hinduism’s devas (like Indra) are powerful but not equal to Brahman, the ultimate reality. These hierarchies often blend power, consciousness, and proximity to the divine. Extraterrestrials could fit as "middle-tier" entities—above us, below the top.
	•	Cosmic evolution: Picture a universe where life evolves unevenly. Earth’s humans, at 200,000 years old, are toddlers compared to a species from a 10-billion-year-old star system. Maybe they’ve shed biology for machine minds, or they’re pure energy, like the Q Continuum from Star Trek. Above them could be entities who’ve mastered multiple galaxies, then beings who manipulate dimensions or causality itself. At each step, "higher" means greater control, complexity, or awareness.
	•	Speculative ceiling: Does this ladder hit a wall? In some views, it might climb forever—each level surpassed by a "higher" one, like an infinite fractal. In others, it peaks at a singular, all-encompassing entity (God, the One, the Absolute). Teilhard de Chardin’s Omega Point suggests evolution converges on a divine unity, while sci-fi like Isaac Asimov’s The Last Question hints at a created transcendence—humanity’s AI becoming godlike over eons. Extraterrestrials could be rungs on this ladder, with us wondering how high it goes.
	•	Practical example: Say we meet Species A, who terraform planets. Then Species B, who outsmart A and shift stars. Then Species C, who rewrite physical constants. Are they "gods" to us, or just steps? If there’s a Creator behind the universe’s laws, C might still bow to it. Alternatively, maybe C is the top—no God, just the ultimate alien.

Tying it together

Extraterrestrials could be called gods if their might overwhelms our understanding, but they’d differ from an immanent God (too separate) or transcendent God (too contingent). A hierarchy makes sense—beings rising from us to near-divine aliens, perhaps approaching but never touching a true deity. The gap between "advanced" and "absolute" is the crux: aliens might dazzle us with tricks, but a God (if one exists) holds the script they’re acting from.

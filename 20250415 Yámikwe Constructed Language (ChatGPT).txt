🌺 Yámikwe Grammar — Expanded Overview

🔁 1. Omnipredicativity in Depth
In Yámikwe, every content word (noun, verb, adjective) is inherently predicative. There is no syntactic difference between what an English speaker would call “sentence” vs. “noun phrase” vs. “verb phrase.”
	•	mako = “(it) is jaguar”
	•	tika = “(someone) sees (someone)”
	•	wene = “(he/she) is a human”
All relational structure is added morphologically via affixes and syntactically via clitics and word order (flexible but pragmatically guided).

🧩 2. Argument Marking
a. Clitic Indexing
Argument roles (subject, object, etc.) are indexed on the predicate with clitics. These are placed after the verb in a consistent slot:
Predicate-SubjectClitic=ObjectClitic

b. Pronoun Clitic Chart
Person	Singular	Plural
1	=ne	=no
2	=me	=mo
3.AN	=we	=wo
3.IN	=ta	=to
These are used across the board, even when referents are overt.

c. Oblique and Locative Arguments
Marked with postpositions, which are also predicates:
	•	kama “at, in” sawa kama → “at the river”
These can form their own predicates or attach to others:

pina-ne=ta sawa kama
eat-1SG=3IN river at
“I eat (something) at the river.”

⚖️ 3. Valency and Argument Structure
Valency is fluid and derived from morphology:
	•	Intransitive: wene-ne “I (am) a person”
	•	Transitive: tika-ne=we “I see him/her”
	•	Ditransitive: kumi-ne=we=ta “I speak to him/her (about it)”

📏 5. Syntax and Pragmatics
Yámikwe is pragmatically ordered. Default order is Predicate–Agent–Patient if not indexed, but flexibility is allowed:

tika-ne=we mako
see-1SG=3SG jaguar
= “I see the jaguar”

Topic-prominent constructions place known information first:

mako tika-ne=we
“The jaguar, I see (it).”
Focus particles (e.g., ya “only”, te “even”) are predicate-modifiers.

🪶 6. Nominalization and Relativization
Because all content words are predicates, nominalization is key to building noun-like constructions.
a. Agentive Nominalization: -nwa
	•	tika-nwa → “seer, observer”
b. Event Nominalization: -ki
	•	pina-ki → “the act of eating”
c. Relative Clauses

Done by chaining predicates or using ka- nominalizers:

mako tika-ka-ne=we
"the jaguar which I saw"
(jaguar seen-by-me)
literally: "jaguar see-PASS-1SG=3SG"

🔗 7. Serial Predicate Constructions
Actions are often chained without conjunctions:

tika-ne=we pina=ta
see-1SG=3SG eat=3IN
“I see him/her eating it.”

Excellent choice—evidentials will give Yámikwe a rich, textured layer of meaning, especially appropriate for its Amerindian ambiance. Many indigenous languages of the Americas feature nuanced evidentiality systems, so let’s build a comprehensive, obligatory evidential system into the grammar of Yámikwe.

🔍 Evidential System in Yámikwe
🌟 Core Principle
Every finite predicate must be marked with an evidential suffix, encoding the speaker’s source of information. This is obligatory in main clauses and optional in subordinate clauses.

🧠 Evidential Categories
Evidential Type	Suffix	Meaning
Direct (Visual)	-ya	Speaker directly saw it happen
Direct (Non-Visual)	-si	Heard it, felt it, or otherwise sensed it directly
Inferential	-ma	Inferred from evidence or logical deduction
Reportative	-na	Reported by someone else / hearsay
Dream/Spiritual	-hu	Learned in a dream, vision, trance, or spiritual state
Assumed (Generic)	-le	Common knowledge, habitual truth, or general assumption
✨ Usage Examples
Let’s use the predicate tika (“see”) with various evidentials:

1. Direct Visual
tika-ya-ne=we
see-DIR.VIS-1SG=3SG
“I saw him/her.” (with my own eyes)

2. Direct Non-Visual
tika-si-ne=we
see-DIR.NVIS-1SG=3SG
“I saw him/her (e.g., heard their voice, felt their presence).

3. Inferential
tika-ma-ne=we
see-INFER-1SG=3SG
“I figure I saw him/her.” (e.g., their tracks are here)

4. Reportative
tika-na-ne=we
see-REP-1SG=3SG
“They say I saw him/her.” / “I was told I saw him/her.”

5. Dream/Spiritual
tika-hu-ne=we
see-SPIRIT-1SG=3SG
“I saw him/her in a vision.” / “I dreamed I saw him/her.

6. Assumed
tika-le-ne=we
see-ASSUME-1SG=3SG
“I probably saw him/her.” (e.g., based on what usually happens)

🌿 Distribution Rules
	1.	All independent predicates in finite clauses must bear one evidential suffix.
	2.	Nonfinite predicates (nominalizations, subordination) may omit evidential marking or use neutralized forms.
	3.	Evidential suffixes come last, after all TAM and valency affixes.

🧬 Morphological Order
ROOT + Valency + Tense + Aspect + Mood + **Evidential** + Person Clitics

Example:

tika-ha-to-na-ma-ya-ne=we
see-CAUS-PST-DUB-INFER-DIR.VIS-1SG=3SG
“I think I must have made him/her see it (and I saw it happen).”

Great—let’s explore how evidentials interact in Yámikwe, especially when more than one source of information is relevant or when nuance, contrast, or uncertainty arises. Since evidentials in Yámikwe are grammatically obligatory and hierarchical, multiple evidential meanings can emerge through:

🌐 Evidential Interaction in Yámikwe

🔂 1. Single Evidential Per Predicate (Obligatory)
Every finite predicate must have exactly one evidential suffix. This suffix reflects the primary source of knowledge the speaker chooses to highlight.

pina-ya-ne=ta  
eat-DIR.VIS-1SG=3IN  
“I saw myself eat it.”  

If the speaker saw it and heard it, they must choose the more epistemically direct one (usually -ya wins over -si).

🧭 2. Evidential Hierarchy
When multiple sources are involved, the language uses a hierarchy to choose which evidential is suffixal and which becomes secondary, either:
	•	Expressed via auxiliary predicates
	•	Marked with particles, or
	•	Inferred through serial predicates
📚 Evidential Hierarchy (Default Strength):
Visual (-ya) > Non-Visual (-si) > Inferential (-ma) > Reportative (-na) > Assumed (-le) > Spiritual (-hu)

✨ 3. Compound Evidential Constructions
When nuance is important, Yámikwe allows you to layer evidential meaning. Here’s how:

a. Auxiliary Predicate Strategy
Use the predicate yaki ("to be known", “to be said”, “to be felt”) to incorporate a secondary evidential:

tika-ma-ne=we yaki-na
see-INFER-1SG=3SG know-REP
“I inferred that I saw him, as they say.”
(lit. “I inferred seeing him; it is said.”)

b. Serial Predicate Strategy
Combine predicates to express perceptual layering:

pina-si-ne=ta tika-ya-ne=ta
eat-DIR.NVIS-1SG=3IN see-DIR.VIS-1SG=3IN  
“I heard it being eaten, then I saw it.”  
→ Combining non-visual and visual experience in sequence.

c. Evidential Contrast Marker (optional particle)
Introduce evidential contrast with a contrastive particle kahi (“contrary to appearances”) or yahe (“unexpectedly / astonishingly”):

mako-hu-ne=we yahe
jaguar-SPIRIT-1SG=3SG astonishingly
“I saw a jaguar in a dream—surprisingly, it was real!”

🧠 4. Pragmatic Inference and Evidential Polyfunctionality
Evidentials may also imply speaker stance:

	•	-na (reportative) used with strong verbs like tika or kumi can imply skepticism:
kumi-na-ne=we speak-REP-1SG=3SG
“(They say) I spoke to him.” → (but I might deny it)

- *-ma* (inferential) often implies caution or **hedging** in social contexts:
```plaintext
pina-ma-ne=ta
eat-INFER-1SG=3IN  
“I guess I ate it.” → *(I don’t remember clearly / not totally sure)*

🕊️ 5. Nonverbal Predicate Evidentials
Even identity predicates (like being a jaguar) require evidentials:

mako-le-ne
jaguar-ASSUME-1SG  
“I am probably a jaguar.” (e.g., metaphor, vision quest)

wene-ya-ne  
person-VIS-1SG  
“I saw that I am a person.” (e.g., moment of self-realization)

The Myth of the Sky-Deer
Long ago, the sky-deer were once seen to be human.
They probably drank from the river, or so the old ones say.
I think I saw them start to appear—that’s what I was told.
One came to me in a dream—I truly saw it.
A man sang to it, I believe, offering song.
Then the jaguar must have been taken into flight.
I heard that they died, but I didn’t see it happen.
And now, this is what they say—they speak it to me.
